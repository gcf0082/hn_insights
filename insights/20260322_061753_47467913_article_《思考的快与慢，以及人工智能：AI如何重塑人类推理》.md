## Key Insights Summary

### 1. Tri-System Theory
The paper introduces **Tri-System Theory**, extending dual-process accounts of reasoning by proposing a third cognitive system:
- **System 1** (in vivo): Fast, Automatic, Intuitive, Associative, Heuristic
- **System 2** (in vivo): Slow, Deliberative, Analytical
- **System 3**: AI-based Artificial Cognition (external algorithmic processing)

### 2. Cognitive Surrender
The paper coins the term **"Cognitive Surrender"** to describe:
> Adopting AI outputs with minimal scrutiny, overriding a user's own intuition and deliberation in a near-total transfer of agency.

### 3. Key Experimental Findings (Three Preregistered Experiments)
- **Accuracy impact**: +25 percentage points when AI was accurate; -15 percentage points when AI erred (Cohen's h = 0.81)
- **Confidence paradox**: Engaging System 3 increased confidence, **even following AI errors**
- **Behavioral pattern**: 20.7% of users showed patterns of substituting AI for emotional support and therapy
- **Usage pattern**: Most users chose to consult AI on a majority of trials (>50%)

### 4. Who Resists Cognitive Surrender?
**Individual Traits:**
- Lower trust towards AI
- High need for cognition (enjoys effortful thinking)
- High fluid intelligence (novel problem-solving capacity)

**Environmental Factors:**
- Receiving detailed feedback
- Operating under certain task conditions

### 5. "Extended Mind" Thesis
The paper tests the "extended mind" thesis about how human intelligence works with machine intelligence. Two modes of working with AI:
- **Centaur Mode**: Human remains in control, AI extends capabilities
- **Cognitive Offloading**: Delegating thinking to AI

## Conclusion

This Wharton research provides empirical evidence that AI (System 3) significantly reshapes human reasoning. The concept of "cognitive surrender" highlights a subtle but significant risk: the "fluency trap" where AI's instant, confident, and grammatically perfect outputs can lead users to override their own intuition and deliberation. However, individual differences (cognitive style, trust levels) and environmental factors (feedback) can mitigate this effect.
